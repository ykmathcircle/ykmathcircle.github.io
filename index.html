Yellowknife Math Circle

The Yellowknife Math Circle is a free program which aims to provide interested students with a space to collaborate, experiment, and to set their own research agendas as junior mathematicians.

For the 2016-2017 school year, two circles are planned, and will (tentatively) be held on alternating Wednesday evenings at Aurora College.

  • For students in grades 5 and 6, the Babbage - Lovelace circle
  • For students in grades 7 and 8, the Ramanujan - Hardy circle

What's a math circle?

'Math circles' originated in Russia and Eastern Europe through the early 1900s, spreading to North America in the 90s. Practices and goals differ from circle to circle, but the uniting theme is that circles make students the drivers of their own understanding by presenting them with open-ended problems or situations whose exploration leads to significant mathematical ideas.

Faced with these problems, students invent and adapt for themselves a number of techniques from areas of 'higher mathematics' such as graph theory, combinatorics, and game theory - necessity being the mother of invention. As they gain experience working through tough problems, students grow in their ability to form, communicate, and critique mathematical arguments. They become intellectually curious and brave - willing to follow through on their ideas about a problem even when they aren't confident that the ideas will produce a solution.

Is this an 'accelleration' program? What sort of student is a good candidate?

Sessions largely side-step the K-12 curriculum, instead providing students with puzzles, games, and problems whose resolutions require creative use of the mathematical skills that they already posess.

The sessions are designed to be challenging for all students, including the strongest. But they are also designed to be very accessible - no special degree of skill with school mathematics is required. Good candidates can be characterized by:

  • a sense of delight at resolving complicated situations
  • curiosity
  • an argumentative streak

What's with the names of the circles?

Our collective understanding of progress in science and mathematics is colored with the idea of the solitary genius who overcomes the misunderstandings or technical limitations of their time (take Einstein as an example). This is, in part, a pragmatic simplification - the 'whole story' of any piece of scientific or mathematical progress would be extremely difficult to tell. Better to have the story of Einstein than no story at all of the emergence of the ideas of modern physics.

It's also a damaging simplification. First, it obscures the rich collaborative processes that give rise to breakthrough ideas, which leads us to de-emphasize communication and collaboration in solving our own problems. Second, it paints a picture of progress where only the superhuman efforts of our best and brightest have any effect, which creates a powerful incentive for the rest of us to disengage, limiting our individual potential and weakening the community of learners which progress depends upon.

To resist these ideas, and to emphasize that matematics is a social enterprise, each circle is named for a great mathematical collaboration.
